What's launching? 🚀

Meet the Long March 10B — a brand-new, 70-metre Chinese heavy-lifter, and this is its very first flight ever.

We're not turning up to some routine satellite launch. July 2026 is the maiden flight of an all-new rocket from China's next-generation fleet. Nobody has watched one of these leave the ground before. We'd be there for #1.

70.2 m tall — about as tall as a Falcon 9
~740 t fully fuelled (≈ 500 cars)
7× YF-100K engines, kerosene + oxygen
8,750 kN liftoff thrust — ~8 jumbo jets
16 t payload to low Earth orbit
♻️ sea partially reusable — booster lands at sea

Why it's a big deal

  • 🥇 Maiden flight — the first time this rocket has ever flown.
  • ♻️ Partially reusable first stage, recovered out at sea — China's answer to SpaceX.
  • 🛰️ Built to haul up China's satellite-internet megaconstellation (their Starlink rival).
  • 🌙 Shares its core with the rocket family that will fly Chinese astronauts to the Moon.
🔊

And the noise. At the pad a launch like this tops 150+ decibels — past the threshold of pain. From our hotel ~5 km up the coast it hits as a delayed, crackling roar you feel in your chest. Sound is slow, so the bang reaches you about 15 seconds after the rocket leaves the pad — you'll see it go, then feel it.

Do I need a visa? 🛂

Almost certainly not. Hainan is China on easy mode.

Hainan is a tropical holiday island with its own relaxed visa policy — it lets in tourists from nearly everywhere visa-exempt, no embassy queue, no paperwork.

On top of that, mainland China currently runs a unilateral visa-free scheme giving most Europeans 30 days of access with nothing to apply for. Between the two, getting in is about as frictionless as China gets.

We'll double-check the exact rules for each passport closer to the time — but for this crew, "just turn up" is the likely answer.

What's the risk? ⚠️

One honest catch: rockets slip. The launch could get pushed back.

The planned launch window is 10–13 July. Mechanical gremlins and weather are completely out of our hands — a scrub and reschedule is always possible, especially on a maiden flight. We can't control the rocket. But we can make sure a slip costs us almost nothing.
